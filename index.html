The Dust Bowl

By: Taha M. and her prevsious History Class

The Dust Bowl is the name applied to a large area in the southern part of the Great Plains region, which suffered extensively from wind erosion during the 1930s. For nearly a decade the dust blew across the Southern Plains. Women hung wet sheets over doorways and windows in an effort to stop dust from invading their homes. Children wore dust masks on their way to school and back. Farmers helplessly watched as their crops blew away.

The areas that were impacted by the Dust Bowl included parts of Kansas, Oklahoma, Texas, New Mexico, and Colorado. In its original state, the region was covered with hardy grasses that held the fine-grained soil in place in spite of the long recurrent (occurring repeatedly) droughts and occasional heavy rains characteristic of the area. A large number of homesteaders (someone who grows crops on land given by the government) settled in the region in the 30 years before World War I, planting wheat and row crops and raising cattle. New machines allowed them to farm more land than ever before. By doing this, the native drought-resistant grasses were replaced with wheat. The surplus of wheat caused the prices to drop. Many fields were left bare. These farming practices left the soil exposed to the danger of erosion by the winds that constantly sweep over the land.

Both man and Mother Nature are responsible for this disaster. Beginning in the early 1930s, the region suffered a period of severe droughts, and the soil began to blow away. When what little rain the area did receive stopped falling, the soil dries very quickly. With no grass to hold the dirt in place, the winds easily picked up the dirt and swirled into dust clouds. The dust clouds, known as “black blizzards,” carried dirt everywhere, even as far as Washington, D.C. and the East Coast. It was impossible to escape it. Dust covered everything.
